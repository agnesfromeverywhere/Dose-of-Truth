Microdosing — taking sub-perceptual doses of psychedelics, typically one-tenth of a full dose — has moved from Silicon Valley productivity hack to a topic of serious academic inquiry.

But the evidence is messier than the headlines suggest.

## What Microdosing Is

A standard microdose of psilocybin mushrooms is typically **0.1 to 0.3 grams** of dried material. The goal is to experience no hallucinations, no significant perceptual changes — just a subtle shift in cognition or mood.

Common protocols involve dosing every third day (the Fadiman protocol) or every other day, over several weeks.

## What the Research Shows

The honest summary: **the evidence is promising but not yet definitive.**

Observational studies consistently show that people who microdose report improvements in mood, focus, creativity, and emotional regulation. A 2021 Imperial College London study tracking nearly 200 microdosers found self-reported improvements across these domains.

But observational data has a fundamental problem: people who choose to microdose believe it will work. That belief alone can produce results (the placebo effect).

The few blinded randomised controlled trials conducted so far have produced mixed results. Some show modest benefits over placebo. Others show no difference.

## Why It Is Hard to Study

Microdosing is uniquely difficult to study rigorously because:

- It is still illegal in most jurisdictions, limiting formal research
- Blinding participants is nearly impossible — people often know whether they took a real dose
- Effects are subtle and self-reported, making objective measurement hard
- Dosing is highly variable across participants

In [The Microdosing Guidebook](https://www.amazon.com.au/dp/0645763209), the approach taken is practical rather than evangelical. The book does not claim microdosing is a cure. It presents what is known, what is uncertain, and how to approach it thoughtfully if you choose to explore it.

## What to Watch

The landscape is changing. As legal frameworks evolve in Australia, Canada, and parts of the US, properly blinded studies are becoming more feasible. Results from several ongoing trials are expected in 2026 and 2027.

The honest position right now: microdosing shows genuine promise, particularly for mood and emotional regulation. But the placebo-controlled evidence is still thin. Watch this space.
